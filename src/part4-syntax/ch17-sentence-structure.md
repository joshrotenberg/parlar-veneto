# Chapter 17: Basic Sentence Structure

Venetian sentence structure largely parallels Italian, with Subject-Verb-Object (SVO) as the default order. However, the obligatory clitic system and certain preposition patterns create distinctive Venetian structures.

## 17.1 Basic Word Order

### Subject-Verb-Object

The default order is SVO, same as Italian:

| Venetian | Italian | English |
|----------|---------|---------|
| *El gato magna el pesse.* | *Il gatto mangia il pesce.* | The cat eats the fish. |
| *Maria compra el pan.* | *Maria compra il pane.* | Maria buys the bread. |
| *I tosi zoga a balon.* | *I ragazzi giocano a pallone.* | The boys play ball. |

### The Clitic Requirement

Unlike Italian, Venetian requires clitic subject pronouns even when a full subject noun phrase is present:

| Venetian | Italian | English |
|----------|---------|---------|
| *Marco **el** magna.* | *Marco mangia.* | Marco eats. |
| *Le tose **le** parla.* | *Le ragazze parlano.* | The girls talk. |
| *El can **el** core.* | *Il cane corre.* | The dog runs. |

The clitic (*el*, *la*, *i*, *le*) is not optional. Saying *Marco magna* without *el* sounds incomplete to Venetian ears.

See Chapter 7 for the full clitic system.

### Null Subjects

First person (singular and plural) can drop the subject pronoun, as in Italian:

| Venetian | Italian | English |
|----------|---------|---------|
| *Magno.* | *Mangio.* | I eat. |
| *Magnemo.* | *Mangiamo.* | We eat. |

But second and third person always need the clitic:

| Venetian | Italian | English |
|----------|---------|---------|
| *Te magni.* | *Mangi.* | You eat. |
| *El magna.* | *Mangia.* | He eats. |

## 17.2 Preposition Patterns

### *In* vs. *A/Al*

One of the most distinctive Venetian features is using *in* where Italian uses *a* or *al*:

| Venetian | Italian | English |
|----------|---------|---------|
| *Vago **in** casa.* | *Vado **a** casa.* | I go home. |
| *Semo **in** bar.* | *Siamo **al** bar.* | We're at the bar. |
| *Rivemo **in** stassion.* | *Arriviamo **alla** stazione.* | We arrive at the station. |
| *El xe **in** cesa.* | *È **in** chiesa.* | He's in church. |

This pattern extends to many location expressions:

| Venetian | Italian | English |
|----------|---------|---------|
| *in piassa* | *in piazza* | in the square |
| *in paese* | *in paese* | in the village |
| *in sità* | *in città* | in the city |
| *in montagna* | *in montagna* | in the mountains |
| *in campagna* | *in campagna* | in the countryside |

Note that some expressions use *in* in both languages, but Venetian extends *in* to contexts where Italian requires *a/al*.

### Evidence from Contemporary Speech

From social media (RUM collaboration):

> *"E quando che rivemo in bar"*
> (And when we arrive at the bar)

Standard Italian would require *al bar*, but Venetian uses *in bar*.

### Preposition Contractions

Venetian contracts prepositions with articles similarly to Italian:

| Preposition + Article | Venetian | Italian |
|-----------------------|----------|---------|
| de + el | *del* | *del* |
| de + la | *dea* / *della* | *della* |
| de + i | *dei* | *dei* |
| de + le | *dee* / *delle* | *delle* |
| a + el | *al* | *al* |
| in + el | *intel* / *nel* | *nel* |
| su + el | *sul* | *sul* |
| par + el | *pel* | *per il* |

Regional variation exists in these contractions.

## 17.3 Topic and Focus

### Topicalization

Like Italian, Venetian can move elements to the front of the sentence for emphasis:

| Neutral | Topicalized | English |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| *Go magnà la torta.* | *La torta, la go magnà.* | The cake, I ate it. |
| *Conosso Marco.* | *Marco, lo conosso.* | Marco, I know him. |

Note that when a direct object is topicalized, a clitic pronoun (*la*, *lo*) remains in the original position.

### Right Dislocation

Elements can also move to the end:

| Neutral | Right-dislocated | English |
|---------|------------------|---------|
| *El gato el dorme.* | *El dorme, el gato.* | He's sleeping, the cat. |

This is common in casual speech for afterthoughts or clarification.

## 17.4 Existential Constructions

### *Ghe xe* (There is/are)

Venetian uses *ghe xe* (sometimes written *ghè* or *ghe'*) for existential statements:

| Venetian | Italian | English |
|----------|---------|---------|
| *Ghe xe un problema.* | *C'è un problema.* | There's a problem. |
| *Ghe xe tanti.* | *Ce ne sono tanti.* | There are many. |
| *No ghe xe gnente.* | *Non c'è niente.* | There's nothing. |
| *Gheto?* | *Ce l'hai?* | Do you have it? |

### *Ghe* as Locative/Dative

*Ghe* also functions as a locative ("there") and dative ("to him/her/them"):

| Venetian | English |
|----------|---------|
| *Ghe vago doman.* | I'm going there tomorrow. |
| *Ghe digo mi.* | I'll tell him. |
| *Ghe porto el vin.* | I'm bringing them the wine. |

## 17.5 Sentence-Final Particles

### *Ciò*

The particle *ciò* (pronounced /tʃɔ/) functions as a filler, attention-getter, or intensifier:

| Venetian | English |
|----------|---------|
| *Varda, ciò!* | Look, hey! |
| *Dai, ciò!* | Come on, man! |
| *Ma ciò...* | But like... |
| *Daghe gas, ciò!* | Floor it, dude! |

*Ciò* instantly marks speech as Venetian. An Italian speaker from Rome or Naples would never use *ciò* this way. It's similar in function to English "man," "dude," or "hey."

### *Mo*

*Mo* (from Latin *modo*) means "now" or adds immediacy:

| Venetian | English |
|----------|---------|
| *Vegno mo.* | I'm coming now. |
| *Falo mo!* | Do it now! |
| *Mo sì!* | Now yes! / Finally! |

### *Po*

*Po* (from *poi*) means "then" or adds a sense of "after all":

| Venetian | English |
|----------|---------|
| *E po?* | And then? / So what? |
| *Vien po!* | Come on then! |

## 17.6 Negation

### Basic Negation with *No*

Venetian negates with *no* (not *non* as in Italian):

| Affirmative | Negative |
|-------------|----------|
| *Magno.* | *No magno.* |
| *Te parli.* | *No te parli.* |
| *El vien.* | *No el vien.* |

*No* precedes the clitic:

- *No te digo gnente.* (I'm not telling you anything.)
- *No i sa.* (They don't know.)

### Double Negation

Like Italian, Venetian uses double negation:

| Venetian | Italian | English |
|----------|---------|---------|
| *No vedo **gnente**.* | *Non vedo **niente**.* | I don't see anything. |
| *No ghe xe **nissun**.* | *Non c'è **nessuno**.* | There's nobody. |
| *No vago **mai**.* | *Non vado **mai**.* | I never go. |

### Negative Words

| Venetian | Italian | English |
|----------|---------|---------|
| *gnente* | *niente* | nothing |
| *nissun* | *nessuno* | nobody |
| *mai* | *mai* | never |
| *gnanca* | *neanche* | not even |

## 17.7 Coordination

### *E*, *O*, *Ma*

Basic coordinators work as in Italian:

| Venetian | English |
|----------|---------|
| *pan **e** vin* | bread and wine |
| *vin **o** bira* | wine or beer |
| *beo **ma** no tropo* | handsome but not too much |

### *Anca*

*Anca* (Italian *anche*) means "also, too":

| Venetian | Italian | English |
|----------|---------|---------|
| *Végno **anca** mi.* | *Vengo **anche** io.* | I'm coming too. |
| *Bala **anca** la sopressa.* | *Balla **anche** la sopressa.* | Even the sopressa is dancing. |

## Summary

### Key Differences from Italian

| Feature | Venetian | Italian |
|---------|----------|---------|
| Clitic subjects | Required: *Marco **el** magna.* | Optional: *Marco mangia.* |
| "At the" (location) | Often *in*: *in bar* | *al*: *al bar* |
| Negation | *no* | *non* |
| "Also" | *anca* | *anche* |
| Existential | *ghe xe* | *c'è* |
| Filler particle | *ciò* | (none) |

### Diagnostic Features

These sentence-level features mark speech as Venetian:
1. Clitic doubling with full subject NPs
2. *In* for locations where Italian uses *a/al*
3. Negation with *no* instead of *non*
4. The particle *ciò*
5. *Ghe xe* for existentials
