# Chapter 5: Nouns

Venetian nouns work much like Italian nouns: they have gender (masculine or feminine) and number (singular or plural). The patterns are similar enough that Italian knowledge transfers well, but with some distinctive Venetian features.

## 5.1 Gender

Every Venetian noun is either masculine or feminine. There is no neuter gender (as in German or Latin).

### Recognizing Gender

**Masculine nouns** typically end in:
- **-o**: *el libro* (the book), *el taolo* (the table), *el fogo* (the fire)
- **-e**: *el pare* (the father), *el nome* (the name)
- Consonant: *el pan* (the bread), *el vin* (the wine), *el can* (the dog)

**Feminine nouns** typically end in:
- **-a**: *la casa* (the house), *la dona* (the woman), *la aqua* (the water)
- **-e**: *la mare* (the mother), *la nose* (the walnut)
- **-ion/-sion**: *la nasion* (the nation), *la passion* (the passion)

### Gender from Italian

Gender usually matches Italian:
- Italian *il libro* (m.) → Venetian *el libro* (m.)
- Italian *la casa* (f.) → Venetian *la casa* (f.)

### Exceptions and Tricky Cases

Some nouns differ from Italian or have unexpected gender:

| Venetian | Gender | Italian | English |
|----------|--------|---------|---------|
| *el sal* | m. | *il sale* | salt |
| *el late* | m. | *il latte* | milk |
| *el mar* | m. | *il mare* | sea |
| *la fin* | f. | *la fine* | end |

### Paired Nouns (Natural Gender)

For people and animals, grammatical gender typically matches natural gender:

| Masculine | Feminine | English |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| *el toso* | *la tosa* | boy / girl |
| *el fiol* / *el fijo* | *la fiola* / *la fija* | son / daughter |
| *el nevodo* | *la nevoda* | nephew / niece |
| *el gato* | *la gata* | male cat / female cat |
| *el can* | *la cagna* | male dog / female dog |

Some nouns use different roots for male/female:
| Masculine | Feminine | English |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| *el omo* | *la dona* | man / woman |
| *el pare* | *la mare* | father / mother |
| *el fradel* / *el fradeo* | *la sorea* / *la soreła* | brother / sister |
| *el nono* | *la nona* | grandfather / grandmother |
| *el marì* | *la mujér* | husband / wife |

## 5.2 Plural Formation

Venetian forms plurals in ways similar to Italian, changing the final vowel.

### Regular Patterns

**Masculine nouns:**

| Singular ending | Plural ending | Example |
|-----------------|---------------|---------|
| -o | -i | *el libro* → *i libri* (books) |
| -e | -i | *el pare* → *i pari* (fathers) |
| consonant | (unchanged or -i) | *el can* → *i cani* (dogs) |

**Feminine nouns:**

| Singular ending | Plural ending | Example |
|-----------------|---------------|---------|
| -a | -e | *la casa* → *le case* (houses) |
| -e | -i | *la mare* → *le mari* (mothers) |

### Examples

| Singular | Plural | English |
|----------|--------|---------|
| *el gato* | *i gati* | cat(s) |
| *el amigo* | *i amighi* | friend(s) |
| *la dona* | *le done* | woman/women |
| *la tosa* | *le tose* | girl(s) |
| *el toso* | *i tosi* | boy(s) |
| *el vecio* | *i veci* | old man/men |
| *la note* | *le noti* | night(s) |

### Spelling Adjustments

To preserve pronunciation, some plurals require spelling changes:

**-co/-go → -chi/-ghi** (to keep /k/ and /g/ sounds):
- *el amigo* → *i amighi* (friends)
- *el lago* → *i laghi* (lakes)
- *el porco* → *i porchi* (pigs)

**-ca/-ga → -che/-ghe**:
- *la barca* → *le barche* (boats)
- *la paga* → *le paghe* (wages)

### Nouns Ending in Consonants

Many Venetian nouns end in consonants (unlike Italian). These often:

**Remain unchanged in the plural** (distinguished by article):
- *el pan* → *i pan* (breads)
- *el vin* → *i vin* (wines)

**Or add -i**:
- *el can* → *i cani* (dogs)
- *el fiol* → *i fioi* (sons)

Regional variation exists here. The article does the work of marking number.

### Irregular Plurals

Some common nouns have irregular plurals:

| Singular | Plural | English |
|----------|--------|---------|
| *l'omo* | *i omini* | man/men |
| *la man* | *le man* | hand(s) |
| *el deo* / *el dito* | *i dei* / *i diti* | finger(s) |
| *el braso* | *i brasi* / *le brasa* | arm(s) |
| *el zenoco* | *i zenoci* / *le zenocje* | knee(s) |
| *l'ovo* | *i ovi* / *le ove* | egg(s) |

Some body parts preserve Latin neuter plurals in -a (now treated as feminine):
- *el braso* (m. sg.) → *le brasa* (f. pl.) - arms
- *el deo* (m. sg.) → *le dea* (f. pl.) - fingers

This is also found in Italian (*il braccio* → *le braccia*).

## 5.3 Diminutives and Augmentatives

Like Italian, Venetian uses suffixes to modify noun meaning:

### Diminutives (smallness, affection)

**-eto/-eta**:
- *el toso* → *el toseto* (little boy)
- *la tosa* → *la toseta* (little girl)
- *el can* → *el caneto* (little dog, puppy)

**-in/-ina**:
- *la casa* → *la casina* (little house)

**-eo/-ea**:
- *el fradel* → *el fradeo* (little brother)

### Augmentatives (largeness, intensity)

**-on/-ona**:
- *el libro* → *el libron* (big book)
- *la casa* → *la casona* (big house)
- *el omo* → *l'omon* (big man)

### Pejorative

**-aso/-asa** (often negative connotation):
- *el tempo* → *el tempaso* (lousy weather)

## 5.4 Compound Nouns

Venetian forms compound nouns similarly to Italian:

**Verb + noun:**
- *el portamonede* (wallet, lit. "carry-coins")
- *el scoassabanchi* (school desk - from *scoassar*, to shake)

**Noun + noun:**
- *el capofameja* (head of family)

**Noun + adjective:**
- *la teracòta* (terracotta)

## 5.5 Nouns from Other Parts of Speech

### Infinitives as Nouns

Like Italian, verbal infinitives can function as nouns (always masculine):
- *El magnar* xe pronto. (The food / eating is ready.)
- *El béver* (drinking, the drink)
- *El saver* (knowledge)

### Adjectives as Nouns

Adjectives can be used as nouns with an article:
- *el vecio* (the old man)
- *la bela* (the beautiful one, the beauty)
- *i giovani* (the young people)

## 5.6 Common Noun Patterns

Some semantic groups tend toward particular endings:

**Trees (often masculine -o or -er):**
- *el perer* (pear tree)
- *el pomèr* (apple tree)
- *el figèr* (fig tree)

**Fruits (often feminine -a):**
- *la pera* (pear)
- *la poma* (apple)
- *la figa* (fig)

**Occupations -or/-ora (like Italian -ore/-rice):**
- *el laorador* / *la laoradora* (worker)
- *el cantador* / *la cantadora* (singer)
- *el pescador* / *la pescadora* (fisherman/woman)

**Places -eria:**
- *la libreria* (bookshop)
- *la panificio* / *la paneteria* (bakery)
- *la osteria* (tavern, wine bar)

## Summary

| Feature | Pattern |
|---------|---------|
| Gender | Masculine (-o, -e, consonant) / Feminine (-a, -e, -ion) |
| Plural m. | -o → -i, -e → -i |
| Plural f. | -a → -e, -e → -i |
| Consonant endings | Often unchanged (article marks number) |
| Diminutive | -eto/-eta, -in/-ina |
| Augmentative | -on/-ona |

Key differences from Italian:
- More nouns ending in consonants (*pan*, *vin*, *can*)
- Plural often marked by article alone when noun ends in consonant
- Some Latin neuter plurals preserved (*le brasa*, *le dea*)
